04 · Kill Gates
What not to rationalize

Each idea gets one unforgiving test.

CompanyKill gateWhy this gate
Threadline 30-day retention and 78%+ SMS gross margin across 10 paid communities. Retention without margin is a vanity metric. Margin without retention is a one-time sale. Both must hit.
ForgePort 3 manufacturers pay $10K-$25K for a neutral-format pilot before proprietary CAD import is built. Paying customers before CAD-kernel work prevents 18 months of engineering chasing the wrong scope.
SceneYield 5 sponsors fund reporting across a portfolio of events before producer SaaS is built. Producer enthusiasm doesn't pay rent. Sponsor checks do. If sponsors won't fund, SaaS is dead-on-arrival.
No Bad Company Three behavioral signals (newsletter response 30%+, event tap rate 50%+, one $10K B2B pre-commit in writing) PLUS senior product CTO co-founder hired full-time at 8-12% equity PLUS eight-principle governance doctrine signed by both founders before any schema. Without all three locks, the platform thesis demands more bandwidth than the founder can provide and more trust than the audience will extend without governance in writing. Any one missing → kill the platform thesis. Run the media business instead.
Which ones should not be built? SceneYield should not be built first as a standalone SaaS — the sponsor-attribution insight is useful but producer-funded subscription software is the wrong default business model. The No Bad Company platform should not be built without a CTO co-founder, governance lock, and behavioral validation — but the No Bad Company media business absolutely should, because that is the actual asset hiding in the brief.

06 · Final Answer
For Zach's friend

The non-templated answer is that the same advice would be wrong for all four.

ForgePort is a high-ceiling enterprise infrastructure company disguised as an asset library. It should only move forward with paid industrial design partners and a strict refusal to become a proprietary CAD-kernel company too early.

Threadline is the strongest first build because the MVP is narrow, the buyer has direct revenue tied to member engagement, and AI can materially improve one operator's throughput.

SceneYield is the one not to build first. It is a sharp cultural insight with a weak default payer. The idea becomes interesting only if sponsor-side money funds the reporting layer before producer SaaS is attempted.

No Bad Company is the one whose right answer surprised the analysis. The platform thesis is the dilution. The media business is the asset. Park the platform until a CTO co-founder is hired and governance is locked. Scale the newsletter to 30K and run brand activations as the B2B wedge. That is a real business — just not the one the brief is pitching.

Answer: build Threadline first. Keep ForgePort as a conditional enterprise bet with paid design partners. Park SceneYield as standalone SaaS — reopen only if sponsor-side money funds it. For No Bad Company: build the media business hard, park the platform thesis, and do not attempt the platform without a CTO co-founder, signed governance doctrine, and three behavioral signals validated inside the existing newsletter + events surface. The right-shaped business hiding in NBC is more like Air Mail than Soho House — and that's a real, fundable, founder-shaped company. The platform fantasy is what would kill it.
